What's the problem and how do we solve it?

BallotReady creates online voter guides that help users research their ballots. I wanted to see how I could use the design process to improve the user experience of researching candidates.

Original Problem Statement

Many BallotReady users are busy urban professionals who spend much of their time on mobile devices. The current BallotReady site is responsive, but not optimized for mobile. Additionally there are features missing, the most important being an easy comparison between candidates.

Hypothesis

Voters would use a mobile app that allows them to easily research candidates while on the go. They would also appreciate having that information stored in their app, integrated with their calendar, and able to send updates and notifications. All these features would make it easier for busy people to complete their ballots and remember to vote.

Findings

  • Most people always vote in presidential elections and all people plan on voting in the next presidential election.
  • Half the people always vote in midterm elections and most plan on voting in the next midterm election.
  • A third of the people always vote in local elections, most sometimes vote, and 1 never votes.
  • A third of the candidates feel that they have enough information to vote on down ballot candidates, half do not feel that they have enough information and another third responded with “other”. The “others” expressed sometimes being confident, feeling that they could know more but haven’t done the proper research, or doing research but not feeling confident in their choices.

Participants used a variety of sources to research candidates. The most popular sources were online news and voter guides, friends and family, and social media. No one gets their information from television, and very few rely on ads or print news.

I selected 5 people from these result as potential interviewees. They all said that they don’t feel they have enough information on down ballot candidates and that they don’t always vote in 2-year or local elections.

Part 2: Interview questions:

  1. You mentioned that you don’t feel you have enough information to make an educated decision on down ballot candidates. Why don’t you have enough information? What obstacles are in your way?
  2. You mentioned that you don’t always vote in local election/ midterm elections. What affects your decision on whether or not to vote?
  3. When you are researching candidates, what information are you looking for?
  4. You mentioned that you choose x,y,z sources for your voting research. Could you tell me some pros and cons of each one of these?
  5. Which resource do you think is the best one? Why?
  6. Which resource do you think is the worst one? Why?
  7. Is there any information you’re looking for that is difficult to find?
  8. Are there ways that information could be presented better?
  9. What do you think would help you become a more informed voter?

Step 3: Analysis

I interviewed three people and asked them the above questions. Several common themes emerged from this research. All the participants felt that they themselves were responsible for not being informed enough around down-ballot issues and candidates. They believed that the information was available, but overwhelming and difficult to synthesize. The interviewees found it difficult to know which sources of information were trustworthy, and each had a system to figure out the level of bias of the media they consumed. Most relied on trusted friends and family, with similar political orientation, to better understand the information. They all thought that if information were better curated or presented, it would help them become more educated voters.

The participants had many ideas on helpful additions to information sources:

  • Donations to candidates
  • Stances over time
  • Summary of offices’ responsibilities
  • How experience relates to the role they’re running for
  • Sources of information/data
  • Increased transparency to show trustworthiness
  • Bullet points of critical information
  • Pros/cons list depending on political leaning
  • Comparison between candidates
  • Other forms of delivery - mobile app/ mixed media
  • Regular emails that send election information to increase personal responsibility

Based on the users I interviewed, I came up with a persona that was a mishmash of them all. Emily the Skeptical Millennial represents a very typical BallotReady user and also the type of user we're trying to reach.

User Stories

Based on my user research, I came up with many potential user stories. In the end, I decided to focus on the feature that I thought would provide the most value, based on the feedback I received: the comparison feature. While there were many potential features that would improve user experience, the comparison feature seemed to be the most common user request and the most straightforward tool that would help users make decisions. It also presented a unique design challenge for mobile.

User Testing

In order to verify my design, I gathered some potential users and asked them to complete a few basic tasks. I learned a lot from watching them interact with the design.

User tasks

  • Identify a candidate’s position
  • Save a candidate to your ballot
  • Navigate to another stance
  • Upvote a candidate
  • Scroll between candidates
  • Filter candidates

Findings

  • Upvote downvote feature was unclear
    • What am I voting for?
    • How many times can I vote?
    • Are my votes cumulative?
  • Too many things happening on the page
    • The design is a bit busy and easy to touch the wrong thing
  • Horizontal scroll is unintuitive
    • Users are accustomed to vertical scroll
  • Stances not grouped or organized
    • It would be better to have topics and subtopics
    • Filtering would best be done by topic, rather than subtopic
  • Too many icons, not enough words
    • Not every icon perfectly communicates the action
    • Add some words for clarity

Style Guide

These colors, typefaces, button, and icons represent a consistent style for the BallotReady comparison feature. I've taken many of the styles from the existing BallotReady site and organized them into a reference document. In the final mockup I changed several elements, most notibly the icons.
